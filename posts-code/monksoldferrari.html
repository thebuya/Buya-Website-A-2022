The Philosophy of the Sages of Nirvana

I have just finished reading Robin Sharmas The Monk who Sold his Ferrari. As I am not an experienced critique of the writings, I will spare you my judgement, However, I do feel compelled to share my awakening from reading the profound wisdom it contains. It has awakened within me the realization that there is something out there.

It's difficult to describe, but this feeling is warm and all-encompassing. I feel connected to everything and everyone around me, in a way that is both powerful and fragile. It's something that I want to cultivate in myself, to hold onto and make a part of my character.

This feeling is familiar to me, as I have experienced it before while reading "The Ethics of Spinoza." I now know that this feeling is real because I have felt it, and I know that I belong with it, just as it belongs in me. I am determined to understand it.

What is it?
It is a warm feeling that washes over me, a sensation of taking everything in while letting everything out. I am aware of our connection - strong, yet fragile. It is unbreakable, yet bendable.

I desire this feeling, and I am determined to have it, and I believe you will to when you are done reading. To attain it, I must stay loyal to the right principles that resonate with my character. The Sage's story of the garden, lighthouse, beads, sunflowers, diamonds, and path will all guide me on this journey. To share a small insight on their meaning;

The Garden is a metaphor for the mind. Just as a garden requires regular care and attention to grow and flourish, so too does our mind. We must nurture it with positive thoughts and actions, and weed out negative thoughts and habits.

The Lighthouse represents our purpose in life. It guides us through the storms and rough waters, providing us with a sense of direction and stability.


Thank you for reading, and special thanks to my friend Adam Alesi, for providing me with the physical copy to read.
